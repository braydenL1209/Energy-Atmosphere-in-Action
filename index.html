Energy from the sun moves through the atmosphere in the form of solar radiation. There are 5 layers of Earth's atmosphere: Troposphere, Stratosphere, Mesosphere, Thermosphere, and Exosphere.

The troposphere extends from the ground to 8-14.5 kilometers high and is where we live and all weather happens. The troposphere contains about 75% of the atmosphere's mass and almost all of the water vapor and the higher you go up in this layer, the cooler and thinner the air gets.

The stratosphere extends from the troposphere to 50 kilometers high. The stratosphere contains the ozone layer, which absorbs harmful ultraviolet rays from the sun. In this layer, the air is calm and most commerical planes fly in the lower part of this layer to avoid weather.

The mesosphere extends from the stratosphere to 85 kilometers high. The stratosphere is the coldest layer of the atmosphere and is where meteors burn up. The mesosphere is the least studied layer of the atmosphere and is too high for planes and baloons but too low for satellites.

The thermosphere extends from the mesosphere to 600 kilometers high. The thermosphere is very hot because the few air molecules in this layer absorb high-energy X-rays and UV radiation from the sun. The temperature can get as high as 2,500°C, but you wouldn't be able to feel it because the air is so thin. The auroras and the International Space Station are located in this layer.

The Exosphere is the outermost layer of Earth's astmosphere and extends from the thermosphere to 10,000 kilometers high. Satellites orbit in this layer and it is where the atmosphere fades into space.

The sun's energy is what drives the water cycle. Water in the oceans, lakes, rivers, and plants are evaporated due to the heat of the sun and rise up in the atmosphere as water vapor. Eventually, the water vapor will condense into clouds. When the water droplets in clouds combine and become heavy enough, they fall back to Earth as precipitation. The water will then runoff back into rivers, oceans, or soaks into the ground to be stored as groundwater.

Weather is the short-term conditions of the atmosphere, like if it is currently raining outside. Climate is the long-term weather patterns of the atmosphere, like the average temperature of an area over 10 years. A climate zone influences weather patterns by determining a region's temperature and precipitation. These zones are primarily defined by geographic location. latitude, altitude, proximity to oceans, and prevailing wind patterns, which collectively shape regional climates.



In a tropical climate, all months have average temperatures greater than 64°F and annual precipitation greater than 59". Moist tropical climates extend north and south from the equator to about 15°-25° latitude.

In dry climates, potential evaporation and transpiration exceed precipitation. These climates extend from 20°-35° north and south of the equator and in large continental regions of the mid-latitudes, often surrounded by mountains.

Moist Subtropical Mid-Latitude Climates generally have warm and humid summers with mild winters. It extends from 30°-50° latitude mainly on the eastern and western borders of most continents. During winter, the main weather feature is the mid-latitude cyclone. Convective thunderstorms dominate summer months.

Moist Continental Mid-Latitude Climates have warm to cool summers and cold winters. The locatioon of these climates extend from 50°-70° latitude. The average temperature of the warmest month is greater than 50°F, while the coldest month average temerature is less than 27°F. Winters are severe, with snowstorms, strong winds, and bitter cold from Continental Polar or Arctic air masses.

Polar climates have year-round cold temperatures, with the warmest month less than 50°F. Polar climates are found on the northern coastal areas of North America, Europe, Asia, and on the land masses of Greenland and Antarctica.

Highlands are unique climates that are a result of elevation. Highland climates occur in mountainous terrain where rapid elevation changes cause rapid climatic changes over short distances.
